MTSS Guidebook

A Framework for Student Success

MTSS Definition

MTSS is an equity driven framework that emphasizes flexible grouping to address the needs of every student through progress monitoring with a primary focus on academic and SEL needs though high quality universal practices and driven tiered support to foster excellence, close learning gaps, and promote accelerated learning options.

CORE VALUES

  • Teaming
  • Fidelity
  • Flexibility
  • Progress Monitoring
  • All Learners

Theory of Action

"If we:"

Prioritize consistent implementation of data driven tiered instruction

"Then we:"

We will see all teachers using high yield instructional strategies to support all learners

"Which will lead to:"

  • Improvement in MAP scores (possibly I-ready)
  • Decrease in behavioral logs
  • Increase in second step task performance

Key Assessment Definitions

Screening

Screening is a brief, standardized assessment administered to all students to identify those who may be at risk for academic difficulties. It serves as an early alert system to ensure timely support and intervention.

Benchmarking Assessments

Benchmarking assessments are conducted at set intervals throughout the school year (typically fall, winter, and spring) to evaluate student performance against grade-level expectations. These assessments provide a broad view of student achievement and help inform instructional planning.

Progress Monitoring

Progress Monitoring involves more frequent assessments for students receiving targeted or intensive support. It tracks a student's growth over time and helps educators determine the effectiveness of interventions and adjust instruction accordingly.

MTSS Non-Negotiables for Schoolwide Success

MTSS Implementation Practices

1. Data-Driven Tiered Instruction:

All teachers consistently use student data to guide instruction and to provide tiered supports at the appropriate level for each learner. Educators regularly analyze formative and summative assessment data to identify students who need additional help or greater challenge, and then deliver Tier 2 or Tier 3 interventions based on those identified needs. This consistent, data-informed approach to tiered instruction ensures each student receives timely support aligned to their needs, in line with the school's MTSS theory of action emphasizing data-driven practices.

2. Flexible Grouping for Targeted Support:

Teachers and interventionists use flexible grouping strategies to address students' evolving needs. Groupings of students are fluid and change based on ongoing assessment results, allowing educators to target specific skill gaps or provide enrichment as appropriate. This means students move in and out of small groups or intervention clusters according to real-time needs. Such flexibility ensures every student gets the right level of support or extension when they need it, reflecting MTSS's commitment to reaching every learner.

3. Universal Screening & Benchmarking:

The school conducts universal academic and SEL screenings for all students at set intervals (e.g. fall, winter, spring) to proactively identify those who may be at risk and those who need additional support. By screening all students regularly, staff can catch emerging academic or behavioral needs early and provide interventions before small issues turn into significant gaps. This schoolwide practice ensures no student "falls through the cracks" unnoticed.

4. Frequent Progress Monitoring of Interventions:

For students receiving Tier 2 or Tier 3 supports, staff consistently implement frequent progress monitoring to track growth and adjust supports. These students are assessed more often (e.g. bi-weekly or monthly) on targeted skills to see if interventions are effective. Teachers and interventionists review progress-monitoring data and promptly revise intervention plans as needed. This continuous monitoring cycle ensures interventions are responsive and leads to better academic and behavioral outcomes for students receiving additional support.

5. High-Quality Universal Instruction:

A non-negotiable in MTSS is strong core instruction for all. Every teacher delivers high-quality, evidence-based Tier 1 instruction that is differentiated to meet diverse learning needs. In practice, this means using high-yield instructional strategies so that the vast majority of students thrive in the general classroom without need for extra intervention. By ensuring Tier 1 is consistently excellent in every classroom, the school fosters overall academic excellence, reduces the number of students needing Tier 2/3 help, and sets all learners up for success from the start.

6. Integrated SEL & Behavioral Supports:

Social-emotional learning (SEL) and behavior supports are fully embedded into the MTSS approach at all tiers. Teachers incorporate SEL instruction and positive behavior supports into daily Tier 1 routines (for example, using a program like Second Step during class meetings) as part of core practice. When students exhibit greater social-emotional or behavioral needs, staff develop targeted Tier 2 or Tier 3 interventions - such as small-group counseling, check-in/check-out plans, or individualized behavior support plans - in addition to academics. Counselors, psychologists, and other specialists collaborate with teachers to implement these supports. By addressing academic and SEL needs together, the school improves overall engagement and reduces behavior incidents (as reflected in outcomes like decreased behavior referrals and improved SEL task completion).

7. Collaborative Data Meetings and Teaming:

Grade-level teams, content-area teams, and student support teams meet regularly to review data and plan student supports as a collective effort. The school schedules protected time (e.g. weekly or biweekly PLC meetings) for educators to engage in structured, data-focused discussions about student progress. In these meetings, teachers, support staff, and interventionists share data insights, diagnose student needs, and make joint decisions on intervention strategies. This consistent teaming and shared decision-making is a non-negotiable: effective MTSS is not carried by any single individual, but rather depends on collaborative, schoolwide effort with ongoing communication and data-informed planning. Such teamwork creates shared responsibility for every student's success and ensures interventions are coordinated across classrooms. Data discussion protocols and agendas are the levers for high quality meetings.

8. Fidelity of Implementation & Documentation:

Staff implement all interventions and support strategies with fidelity and document them thoroughly. When an intervention plan is put in place, teachers and interventionists carry it out exactly as designed (in terms of duration, frequency, and instructional strategies) to ensure its integrity. They also use shared tools such as intervention logs, progress trackers, or meeting notes to record what supports are provided, student progress updates, and outcomes. MTSS teams routinely review these records to verify interventions are being delivered consistently and as intended, essentially "tracking fidelity" of implementation. This transparency and accountability allow the school to evaluate what's working and ensure no planned support slips through the cracks.

9. Continuous Data-Driven Improvement:

MTSS is a continuous improvement process rather than a one-time initiative. Teachers and teams regularly reflect on student outcome data and the effectiveness of their instruction/interventions, using that information to inform next steps. In practice, this means no one sticks with an ineffective strategy out of habit - instead, staff engage in ongoing problem-solving, and if data shows a strategy isn't yielding progress, they adjust or try a new approach. For example, at the Tier 3 level, MTSS teams conduct frequent reviews of individual student data to determine whether to intensify support or change tactics. This relentless, data-driven reflection and adjustment cycle ensures that the MTSS framework improves over time and remains responsive to student needs, ultimately leading to better academic and behavioral outcomes for all students.

MTSS Classroom Practices

Non-Negotiable MTSS Practices for Classrooms

  • Deliver High-Quality, Differentiated Tier 1 Instruction: Instruction must meet diverse learner needs and be rooted in standards-based practices.
  • Post and Communicate Academic and SEL Expectations: Use visual aids and consistent messaging around objectives and behaviors.
  • Use Formative and Summative Assessments Regularly: Assessments should inform grouping, reteaching, and student feedback.
  • Maintain a Classroom Data Tracking System: Track student progress across multiple assessments (formative, summative, diagnostic).
  • Implement Flexible Grouping Structures: Regularly regroup students based on data and needs for reteaching or enrichment.
  • Provide Tier 2 Reteaching Opportunities in Class: Small-group, targeted support for students not meeting grade-level benchmarks.
  • Provide Tier 3 Intensive Supports with Fidelity: Use individualized interventions with higher frequency and documented progress.
  • Set and Communicate Thresholds for Mastery: Students must clearly understand what success looks like.
  • Offer Reassessment Opportunities and Feedback: Allow students to revisit assessments after feedback or reteaching.
  • Engage Students in Goal Setting: Use student data to co-develop goals and progress monitoring.
  • Integrate SEL Practices into Daily Routines: Morning meetings, check-ins, and restorative circles are part of the instructional day.
  • Document and Communicate Supports: Maintain logs of interventions and communicate with families when students need support.
